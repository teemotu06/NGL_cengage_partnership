The Paradox

The spend is world-leading.
The outcomes are not.

Research shows that phonics instruction in Korean national curriculum textbooks is inconsistent, limited in scope, and varies significantly from textbook to textbook. English doesn't start until Grade 3, with just two hours per week. Yet families invest in private English education earlier and more heavily than almost anywhere else.

The Root Cause

The gap is not effort.
It is how English is taught at the foundation.

Peer-reviewed research identifies a specific challenge for Korean-speaking children learning to read in English. Standard EFL materials — whether international or Korean-made — don't fully address it.

Literacy skills do not carry over from Korean to English.

Korean children develop strong phonological awareness — the ability to hear and manipulate sounds. But the ability to connect letters to sounds is specific to each writing system. English requires explicit, systematic instruction that Korean's transparent Hangul does not.

Wang, Park & Lee (2006), Journal of Educational Psychology

Without systematic phonics, progress stalls.

A controlled study found that EFL learners without explicit phonics instruction made "very limited progress (or even none at all) in phonological decoding, even over extended periods."

Li & Woore (2021), System

Even Korean-made phonics books leave outcomes dependent on teacher quality.

Most Korean phonics series (Smart Phonics, Oxford Phonics World, and others) follow a standard 5-book structure: learn A–Z, then short vowels, then long vowels, then blends and digraphs. This structure works for publishers — but it relies heavily on individual teacher knowledge. Two teachers using the same book can produce completely different student outcomes, because the book provides content but doesn't control the instructional method. One teacher might know to explicitly address Korean-specific patterns like /r/ and /l/ confusion; another might not. LUMINOS is designed to be the instruction itself — the system delivers the methodology, and the teacher becomes the coach.

The Framework Gap

Our World Starter maps to CEFR Pre-A1 — but Pre-A1 still assumes emergent literacy readiness: awareness of sound-symbol relationships, alphabet knowledge, basic vocabulary, and print conventions. Korean children entering English at ages 3–7 typically arrive without these foundations. They are pre-literate in English.

Observed in the Classroom

At Explorers Academy (Incheon), new enrolments consistently arrived unable to access Our World Starter or Level 1 — not due to ability, but because they lacked the decoding skills these materials presuppose. Single-site observation — consistent with peer-reviewed predictions.

While Our World Phonics with ABC provides foundational phonics support, the depth and structure needed for Korean EFL learners in hagwon settings — particularly the systematic phoneme sequencing, Korean L1 interference targeting, and semester-length programme design — goes beyond what a supplementary series is designed to deliver.
