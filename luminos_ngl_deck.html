The Root Cause

The gap is not effort. It is how English is taught at the foundation.

Literacy skills do not transfer from Korean to English

Korean children hear sounds well — but connecting letters to sounds is specific to each writing system. English requires explicit instruction that Korean's transparent Hangul does not.

Wang, Park & Lee (2006), J. of Educational Psychology

Without systematic phonics, progress stalls

EFL learners without explicit phonics instruction made "very limited progress (or even none at all) in phonological decoding, even over extended periods."

Li & Woore (2021), System

Even Korean-made phonics books leave outcomes dependent on teacher quality. Most series follow a standard 5-book structure (A–Z → short vowels → long vowels → blends → digraphs). This provides content — but doesn't control the instructional method. Two teachers using the same book can produce completely different outcomes. LUMINOS is designed to be the instruction itself. The teacher becomes the coach.

Korean L1 interference: Choi (2016); de Jong, Hao & Park (2009); Sheldon (1985)

The Framework Gap

Our World Starter maps to CEFR Pre-A1 — but Pre-A1 assumes emergent literacy readiness: sound-symbol awareness, alphabet knowledge, basic vocabulary, print conventions. Korean children entering English at ages 3–7 typically arrive without these foundations.

Observed in the Classroom

At Explorers Academy (Incheon), new enrolments consistently couldn't access Our World Starter or Level 1 — not due to ability, but because they lacked the decoding skills these materials presuppose. Single-site observation — consistent with peer-reviewed predictions.

While Our World Phonics with ABC provides foundational phonics support, the depth and structure needed for Korean EFL learners in hagwon settings — particularly systematic phoneme sequencing, L1 interference targeting, and semester-length programme design — goes beyond what a supplementary series is designed to deliver.
